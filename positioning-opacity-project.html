VOLCANOES

What are Volcanoes and How do they form?

A volcano is an opening in the Earth’s crust where molten rock, ash, and gases can escape from deep underground. Beneath the surface, intense heat melts rock into a liquid called magma, which collects in a magma chamber. When pressure builds up, the magma pushes upward through cracks and if the pressure becomes strong enough, the volcano erupts, releasing lava, ash clouds, and hot gases onto the surface.
Most volcanoes form along the edges of tectonic plates, which are huge pieces of the Earth’s crust that slowly move. Volcanoes often appear where plates collide, pull apart, or slide over each other. They can also form above hotspots, which are areas where extremely hot magma rises from deep inside the mantle. Volcanoes exist on land and under the ocean, and their shape and behaviour depend on the type of magma beneath them.











Different Types of Volcanoes

Shield Volcanoes

  • Shield volcanoes have wide, gentle slopes because their lava is very runny and spreads far before cooling.
  • They are mainly made from basaltic lava, which flows easily and builds a low, shield-shaped mountain.
  • Their eruptions are usually gentle and frequent, producing steady lava flows.
  • Examples include Mauna Loa and Kīlauea in Hawaii.

Composite Volcanoes (Stratovolcanoes)

  • Composite volcanoes are tall, steep-sided volcanoes built from layers of ash, lava, and rock.
  • Their magma is thick and traps gas, causing powerful explosive eruptions.
  • They are commonly found at destructive plate boundaries where plates collide.
  • Examples include Mount Fuji, Mount Vesuvius, and Mount St. Helens.

Cinder Cone Volcanoes

  • Cinder cone volcanoes are small, steep cones made from ash, cinders, and rock fragments.
  • They form quickly when particles from an eruption fall back around the vent.
  • Their eruptions are short-lived but explosive, releasing lots of gas and ash.
  • Many cinder cones appear on the sides of larger volcanoes.

Lava Dome Volcanoes

  • Lava domes form when thick, sticky lava piles up near the vent instead of flowing far.
  • The lava’s thickness can trap pressure, leading to sudden explosive eruptions.
  • Lava domes grow slowly and can become very large over time.
  • They often form inside the craters of composite volcanoes after major eruptions.

Submarine Volcanoes

  • Submarine volcanoes form under the ocean, usually along mid-ocean ridges where plates pull apart.
  • Most eruptions are hidden underwater but still release huge amounts of lava.
  • Some submarine volcanoes grow tall enough to form new islands.
  • They are the most common type of volcano on Earth.

Caldera Volcanoes

  • Calderas form when a massive eruption empties the magma chamber and the ground collapses inward.
  • This collapse creates a huge, bowl-shaped depression on the surface.
  • Caldera eruptions are extremely powerful and can affect global climate.
  • Famous calderas include Yellowstone and Crater Lake.

Fissure Volcanoes

  • Fissure volcanoes erupt from long cracks in the Earth’s crust instead of a single vent.
  • They release runny lava that spreads out to form wide lava plateaus.
  • Their eruptions are usually gentle but can cover huge areas with lava.
  • Iceland has many famous fissure eruptions, such as the Laki fissure.

Complex Volcanoes

  • Complex volcanoes have more than one vent, creating irregular shapes and overlapping cones.
  • Their eruptions can vary, producing both lava flows and explosive ash clouds.
  • They often form when the main vent becomes blocked and new vents open nearby.
  • Complex volcanoes can be unpredictable because of their changing structure.

How Volcanoes Erupt:

Volcanic eruptions happen when pressure inside the Earth becomes too strong for the crust to hold. Gas bubbles in the magma expand, pushing it upwards until it bursts out of the vent. Some eruptions are gentle, with lava flowing slowly down the sides of the volcano. Others are explosive, sending ash clouds, rocks, and hot gases high into the sky. The type of eruption depends on the magma’s thickness and gas content. Thick, sticky magma traps gas and causes violent eruptions, while runny magma allows gas to escape more easily.

Effect of Volcanoes

Volcanoes can have both destructive and beneficial effects on people, the environment, and the atmosphere. When a volcano erupts, it can release huge amounts of ash, lava, and gases that destroy everything in their path, wiping out homes, forests, and farmland in seconds. Ash clouds can block sunlight, disrupt air travel, and cause breathing problems for people and animals, while fast‑moving pyroclastic flows are extremely dangerous and can bury entire towns. However, volcanoes also create new land, enrich soil with minerals, and support farming in the long term because volcanic ash eventually breaks down into very fertile soil. Some eruptions even form new islands or expand coastlines. Volcanic gases like sulfur dioxide can cool the Earth temporarily by reflecting sunlight, while lava flows can reshape landscapes and create natural features such as hot springs and geysers. Although volcanoes can cause loss of life, economic damage, and long‑term environmental change, they also play an important role in building the Earth’s surface and supporting ecosystems.
