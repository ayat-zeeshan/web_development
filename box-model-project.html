Tokyo Tower: Japan's Iconic Landmark

Tokyo Tower is one of the most recognisable symbols of Japan, rising high above the city with its striking red and white steel frame. Completed in 1958, it was built during a time when Japan was rebuilding and modernising after the war, and the tower quickly became a proud representation of the country’s new identity. Inspired by the Eiffel Tower but made even taller, Tokyo Tower was designed not only as a broadcasting tower but also as a landmark that would show the world Japan’s growing confidence and technological progress.

Today, the tower continues to play an important role in Tokyo’s skyline. During the day, it stands out brightly against the sky, but at night it becomes even more magical, glowing with warm orange lights that can be seen from miles away. Visitors from all over the world come to explore its observation decks, where they can look out over the endless cityscape of Tokyo — from the busy streets of Shibuya to the peaceful gardens around the Imperial Palace. With shops, restaurants, and even a small aquarium inside the building, Tokyo Tower is more than just a structure; it’s a full experience that captures the energy, beauty, and spirit of the city. Whether you're interested in its history, its design, or simply the incredible views it offers, Tokyo Tower remains one of the most unforgettable plces to visit in Japan.

History

Tokyo Tower was completed in 1958, during a period when Japan was rapidly rebuilding and transforming itself after the Second World War. The country wanted a structure that would symbolise its new era of growth, ambition, and technological progress, and the idea of a tall broadcasting tower became the perfect solution. Inspired by the Eiffel Tower in Paris, the designers created a structure that was not only similar in shape but actually taller, reaching an impressive 333 metres. This height made Tokyo Tower the tallest structure in Japan at the time, and it quickly became a national symbol of pride. The tower was originally built to support television broadcasting, which was becoming increasingly popular across Japan. More than 4,000 tonnes of steel were used in its construction, and interestingly, much of the metal came from American military tanks left behind after the war. Its bright red and white colours were chosen to meet aviation safety regulations, but they also gave the tower a unique and instantly recognisable appearance. Over the decades, Tokyo Tower has survived earthquakes, storms, and the arrival of newer skyscrapers, yet it remains one of the most iconic landmarks in the country. Even today, it continues to broadcast signals, attract visitors, and represent the spirit of Tokyo — a city that blends tradition, innovation, and resilience.

Fun Facts!

  • Tokyo Tower is painted every 5 years, and it takes around 1 year to repaint the entire structure. The colours must follow strict aviation rules: bright international orange and white so planes can see it clearly.
  • Even though it looks extremely modern, Tokyo Tower was built in 1958, which makes it older than many of the skyscrapers around it today. It has survived earthquakes, storms, and huge changes in the city.
  • The tower is 333 metres tall, which is 13 metres taller than the Eiffel Tower, the structure that inspired its design.
  • More than 4,000 tonnes of steel were used to build it — and surprisingly, some of that steel came from American military tanks left behind after the Korean War.
  • At night, Tokyo Tower lights up in different colours depending on the season or event. For example, it glows warm orange in winter and cool white in summer. Sometimes it even lights up in special colours for celebrations or awareness campaigns.
  • The tower has two observation decks: the Main Deck at 150m and the Top Deck at 250m. From the top, you can see Mount Fuji on a clear day.
  • Inside the tower, there are shops, restaurants, a small shrine, and even a footstep glass floor where you can look straight down at the city below.
  • Tokyo Tower is still used for broadcasting today, even though Tokyo Skytree is taller. It continues to send radio and digital signals across the city.
